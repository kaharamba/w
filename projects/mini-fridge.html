Reverse Brayton-Cycle Mini-Fridge — Concept Design

Completed as part of ME2331: Introduction to Design

Project Overview

This project developed a preliminary concept for a compact 12×12 mini-fridge based on a reversed Brayton cycle using air as the working fluid. The system architecture integrates a small centrifugal compressor, heat exchanger, turbo pump, and fan to enable rapid heat extraction without relying on refrigerants or consumable materials.

The Brayton-cycle approach was selected over solar, ice-based, and thermoelectric alternatives due to its low environmental impact, minimal setup requirements, and potential for fast transient cooling. A structured trade study using Pugh's Matrix identified the Brayton-cycle concept as the highest-scoring option when compared against a vapor-compression mini-fridge baseline, despite acknowledged manufacturing challenges.

Design Requirements

The concept was developed to meet the following system-level requirements:

  • Accommodate cylindrical beverage containers up to 3 in diameter × 8 in length
  • Support containers made of plastic, metal, or glass
  • Enable active cooling of up to two beverages simultaneously
  • Reduce beverage temperature from 80°F to 36°F within 7 minutes
  • Prevent overcooling below 30°F
  • Operate without specialized consumables beyond those commonly found in a kitchen
  • Maintain an overall system envelope of 18 inches cubed or smaller
  • Require less than 5 minutes of setup time
  • Target a manufacturing cost below $300 (2022 USD)
