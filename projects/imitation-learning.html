Imitation Learning Model for Day–Night Autonomous Driving

Status: Extended abstract completed; poster presentation delivered; full manuscript in preparation.

Objective & Research Summary

This project investigates a simplified end-to-end imitation learning approach for low-level autonomous vehicle control under varying illumination conditions. Using a go-kart–based autonomous platform equipped with RGB and thermal cameras, convolutional neural networks were trained to map sensor inputs directly to steering and throttle commands. While daytime performance exceeded 95% accuracy, nighttime performance revealed significant degradation due to distribution shift, motivating future work in conditional imitation learning and hierarchical control architectures.

Key Contributions

  • Designed and deployed a multimodal autonomous vehicle platform (RGB + thermal)
  • Collected and curated a 50k+ image dataset across day and night conditions
  • Trained and evaluated CNN-based imitation learning models for low-level control
  • Identified failure modes caused by illumination-induced distribution shift
  • Proposed architectural extensions using conditional policies and high-level commands

Results & Failure Analysis

The go-kart autonomously navigated 0.9 miles at night using a thermal-camera-based imitation learning model, requiring four human interventions when the vehicle deviated from the road. Notably, the model was trained using data collected from a single manual driving pass.

In contrast, the RGB-based model performed significantly worse under nighttime conditions, repeatedly steering toward brightly illuminated grass patches. This behavior was attributed to dataset bias and poor generalization under low-light conditions.
